            # 2. Simulate the merge

Why it HELPS (The "Shortcut" Principle)
A neural network can eventually learn to calculate poker odds and outs from raw cards, but it takes millions of iterations to learn the math of a 52-card deck.
By calculating the hand strength and outs yourself and feeding them directly into the Input Layer, you are giving the network a massive shortcut. How it can HURT (The "Information Destruction" Trap)
It only hurts if you feed the network only your abstracted data and remove the raw cards from the input.
If you only tell the network "My hand strength is 85%", you have destroyed the concept of Card Removal (Blockers).

Example: Having an 85% hand strength with the Ace of Spades is strategically different than having an 85% hand strength without it, because holding the Ace of Spades physically blocks your opponent from having a Spade flush.

The Solution: Always feed the network both. Feed it the raw cards (so it can learn blockers) and your engineered features (hand strength, outs) so it gets the mathematical shortcut.

2. What other NN architectures are beneficial for RL, especially for N-Player games?
While the Hourglass (Autoencoder) shape is great for state abstraction, RL agents usually require slightly different wiring to handle the element of time and multiple actors. Here are the three architectures actually used in cutting-edge RL:

A. Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs or GRUs)
The Problem with standard MLPs (Option C): A standard feed-forward network has no memory. It only looks at the current state. In poker, you need to remember that your opponent hesitated and checked on the flop, even if you are now on the river.

The Architecture: You use an LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) layer right after your bottleneck. As the game progresses through betting rounds, the LSTM keeps a "running hidden state" (a memory) of the entire sequence of bets and actions, allowing the agent to understand long-term betting narratives.

B. The Two-Headed Actor-Critic (Standard RL)
The Concept: Almost all modern Deep RL (like PPO, which powers ChatGPT's RLHF, or advanced game bots) uses this.

The Architecture: The network has one shared bottleneck trunk (to understand the game state), but splits into two distinct heads:

The Actor Head: Outputs the probability of actions (Fold: 10%, Call: 60%, Raise: 30%).

The Critic Head: Outputs a single number predicting the Value of the state (e.g., "I expect to win 2.5 chips from this position").

Why it works: The Critic acts as a coach for the Actor, adjusting the Actor's strategy during training based on whether the actual reward matched the predicted value.

C. Transformers / Attention Mechanisms (For N-Player Games)
The Problem with N-Player Games: Standard neural networks hate variable input sizes. If you build a network for 6-player poker, you might create 6 input slots. But what happens when 3 people fold? You have to fill those slots with zeroes (padding), which confuses the network. Furthermore, a standard network cares about the order of inputs, but strategically, "Opponent A raising" is often the same threat as "Opponent B raising".

The Architecture: You use Self-Attention (the same architecture behind LLMs like Gemini).

Why it works for N-Players: Attention mechanisms don't care how many inputs there are. You just feed it a list of "Active Players and their actions." The Architecture Blueprint: "Manager-Worker"
You will split your agent into two completely separate neural networks.

1. The Manager (The N-Player Macro Agent)
The Goal: Analyze the entire global state, evaluate all N players, and decide on a single, high-level strategic objective (e.g., "Attack Player 3," "Retreat to Base," "Farm Resources").

The Architecture: Transformer / Self-Attention Network. * Because it’s an N-player Free-For-All (FFA), players will die, drop out, or be out of vision. A standard neural network cannot handle this variable input size.

You feed the Manager a list of [Player_1_State, Player_2_State, ..., Player_N_State]. The Attention mechanism dynamically calculates the relationships between everyone on the board.

The Output: A discrete objective vector. It outputs a Target_Action (e.g., Attack) and a Target_Entity_ID (e.g., Player 3).

2. The Worker (The 1v1 Execution Agent)
The Goal: Take the objective from the Manager and execute the raw physical inputs (steer left, shoot, cast spell, dodge) to accomplish it.

The Architecture: Feed-Forward MLP or LSTM.

The Secret Sauce: The Worker does not see the N-player game. When the Manager says "Attack Player 3," you run a script that completely masks out all other players. You feed the Worker a highly compressed, fixed-size input: [My_State, Player_3_State, Environment_Geometry].

The Output: Raw mechanical actions (joystick movements, button presses).

Why this is a massive advantage
Massive State Space Reduction: If you have 10 players, the math for collision, targeting, and threat assessment scales exponentially. By forcing the Worker to only look at 1 or 2 targets at a time, its neural network can be incredibly small, fast, and highly optimized for 1v1 mechanics.

Modular Training (Curriculum Learning): You don't have to train this massive N-player beast all at once.

Phase 1: You can train the Worker in a sterile 1v1 sandbox. You hardcode the goal ("Kill this dummy") and let it perfect its mechanical execution.

Phase 2: You freeze the Worker's brain. You put it in the FFA arena and train only the Manager. The Manager learns that if it points the Worker at a weak player, the Worker will get the kill.

The Two Major Traps (and how to fix them)
If you build this, you will immediately run into two specific problems that plague HRL systems:

Trap 1: The "Blind Gank" Problem
If the Worker is only looking at Player 3, what happens if Player 4 sneaks up behind you with a shotgun? The Worker is completely blind to it.

The Fix (Interrupt Signals): The Manager must run at a lower frequency than the Worker. For example, the Worker executes actions 30 times a second, but the Manager evaluates the global board 2 times a second. If the Manager sees Player 4 approaching, it instantly sends an "Interrupt/Override" signal to the Worker, changing the target to Player 4 or changing the objective to "Flee."

Trap 2: The "Impossible Handoff"
The Manager looks at the global map and says, "Attack Player 2." But Player 2 is behind a massive, unbreakable wall. The Worker receives the command, runs into the wall repeatedly, and gets stuck. The Manager blames the Worker for failing, and the neural weights get corrupted.

The Fix (Navigation Mesh / Validity Masking): Do not let the Manager output impossible goals. Before the Manager's outputs are finalized, you apply an "Action Mask." You run a quick line-of-sight or pathfinding check. If Player 2 is unreachable, you mathematically force the Manager's probability of choosing "Attack Player 2" to exactly 0.0. It is forced to pick a valid strategy.

You have the right intuition: it is absolutely about taking long-term future rewards into account. But in the context of Reinforcement Learning (and specifically in David Abel’s lecture), "Planning" and the "Speed of Planning" have very rigid, mathematical definitions.

He is not talking about a real-time search (like an AI "thinking" during a live game of Chess or Poker). He is talking about solving the environment offline using an algorithm called Value Iteration.

Here is exactly what "planning" means in this part of the video, and how abstraction speeds it up.

1. What is "Planning" in this context?
In Reinforcement Learning, there is a strict divide between Learning and Planning:

Learning: The agent doesn't know the rules. It runs around blindly, bumps into walls, finds a reward, and updates its strategy through trial and error.

Planning: The agent is handed a perfect map of the world (the exact transition probabilities and reward functions—an MDP). It doesn't need to do trial and error; it just runs a mathematical algorithm to calculate the perfect strategy (the optimal Value Function) for every single square on the map before the game even starts.

In the video, David Abel defines the "Planning Problem" specifically as running the Value Iteration algorithm until it finds the optimal expected long-term reward for every state.

2. How does Value Iteration normally work? (Slow Planning)
Imagine a 10x10 grid maze with a piece of cheese at the top right.
Value Iteration works by sweeping across the entire board, one step at a time, backing up the reward.

Iteration 1: The square right next to the cheese realizes, "Hey, if I step right, I get 10 points!" It updates its value to 10. Every other square is still 0.

Iteration 2: The square two steps away realizes, "Hey, if I step right, I land on the 10-point square!" It updates its value to 9 (discounted for taking a step).

Iteration 15: It takes 15 full sweeps over the entire board for the value of the cheese to "flow" all the way back to the starting square at the bottom left.

This step-by-step propagation of long-term reward backward through the tree is why standard planning is slow.

3. What is the "Speed of Planning"? (Fast Planning via Options)
In the second half of the video, Abel introduces Action Abstraction (specifically, the "Options" framework). An Option is a macro-action, like "Navigate directly to the nearest doorway."

Abel defines the "Speed of Planning" strictly as the number of iterations it takes for Value Iteration to finish.

If you add an Option to the game, you create a "long-horizon edge" (a shortcut) in the game tree.

Instead of the reward taking 5 iterations to slowly seep backward through a 5-tile hallway, the Option says, "If you are at the start of the hallway, you can instantly warp to the end."

Because the algorithm evaluates this macro-action, the reward from the cheese jumps across the entire hallway in Iteration 1.

Summary
When they talk about the "Speed of Planning," they are asking: "If we group multiple small actions into big abstract actions (Options), how many fewer computational loops will it take for the algorithm to calculate the optimal long-term rewards for the entire game?"

The answer is that it takes far fewer iterations, but Abel's mathematical proof later in the video reveals a harsh truth: actually figuring out which abstract actions are the best to add is an NP-Hard problem, meaning it often takes more compute to find the perfect macro-actions than it does to just run the slow planning algorithm in the first place!
